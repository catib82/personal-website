For centuries, the obsession of art has been to imitate reality and, over time, define a stable perspective of the world through ​​representation. A positivist perspective that has relied on figuration and that has established a specific visual regime. Photography could have been a tool based solely on mimicry, strengthening the idea that its results are the only possible representation of the world. But the medium has managed to find multiple cracks through which we can find the potential for change. Photography makes visible, precisely, the impossibility of representation, and thus becomes, rather than the result of a technical process around the image, a theoretical object [1], capable of destabilizing representation and reordering the world.

This body of work combines the series Form (2019) and Chemicals (2019) by Cati Bestard (Inca, Mallorca, 1982). It is an exercise that explores the capacity of success found in error, as well as the potential for transformation and the limits of the photographic medium. In both series, Bestard works with expired Instant Fuji Film, a total of seven rolls of film with 10 exposures each, that some friends left to her when they vacated their studio in New York. In Form the images are shot on-camera, and the results correspond to the performance capabilities of the expired chemicals: sometimes we can see a glimpse of reality, and in others, the chemicals adopt different shapes and colors. In Chemicals, the instant film does not respond inside the camera, as the chemicals were too dry. In these cases, Bestard has manipulated the material directly with her hands, spreading the chemical across the surface and thus making the dysfunctionality of the material visible. As a whole, we are looking at images found in a liminal and phantasmagorical space, a place that has the capacity to question binary or dialectical logics between ideality and act [2]. Both in Form and Chemicals, the results derail from the original intention of the material [3], a fact that, without a doubt, gives Bestard's work a queer potential that we find in various phases of the process: a transferred material, expired, and the legitimization or visibility of an unexpected result, unpredictable and deviant.

In this work there is a certain rejection of pragmatism and, consequently, some resistance to rationality. Rejection, resistance, failure, error. While these ideas can connote negativity, far from being so, in Bestard's work, the aesthetic of queer failure is purposeful. In it we find "a form of virtuosity that helps viewers get out of the static world [4]".
